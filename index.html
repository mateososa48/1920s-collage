1920s Collage

The Roaring Twenties

The 1920s were a decade of jazz, fast change, fame, excitement, and conflict. This collage brings together ten images that show both the energy of the time and the tensions underneath it.

01

Jazz Age Musicians

These musicians represent how important jazz was in the 1920s. Jazz was one of the clearest symbols of this decade because it felt new, energetic, and very different from older styles of music. Artists like Louis Armstrong and Duke Ellington helped make jazz popular across the country, not just in Black communities where it had started and grown. Their music shaped the mood of the 1920s and made this era feel exciting and modern.

02

The Cotton Club

The Cotton Club was one of the most famous nightclubs of the 1920s. It was located in Harlem and became known for jazz music, big performances, and also a very flashy nightlife atmosphere. A lot of major Black performers played there, which helped spread jazz to a larger audience, beyond black communities. At the same time, it also showed the racism of the period, because Black entertainers performed for mostly white crowds. this makes it important not just as a music venue, but also symbolizes the contradictions of the decade.

03

A Flapper

The flapper became one of the most recognizable images of the 1920s. Flappers were young women who pushed back against older expectations by wearing shorter dresses, cutting their hair short, and acting more openly and independently in public. They represented a big cultural shift, especially after women gained the right to vote in 1920. This image matters because it shows how the decade was not just about money and parties, but also about changing gender roles and new ideas about freedom.

04

Scopes Trial Political Cartoon

This political cartoon connects to the Scopes Trial, which became one of the most famous court cases of the 1920s. The case started when John Scopes was accused of teaching evolution in a Tennessee public school, which was against state law. What made the trial so important was that it turned into a national argument over science, religion, and education. This cartoon shows how people saw the trial as part of a much bigger fight between modern thinking and traditional beliefs.

05

Ku Klux Klan in the 1920s

This image shows the Ku Klux Klan during its major comeback in the 1920s. A lot of people think of the KKK mainly in the Reconstruction era, but it actually became powerful again in this decade. The group grew because of racism, fear of immigrants, anti-Catholic prejudice, and the belief that America was changing too fast. Its resurgence shows that the 1920s were not only a time of growth and entertainment, but also a time of hate, exclusion, and backlash against social change.

06

Teapot Dome Scandal Political Cartoon

This cartoon represents the Teapot Dome Scandal, one of the biggest political scandals of the 1920s. The scandal happened when government oil reserves were secretly leased to private companies in exchange for bribes. It became a major example of corruption in President Harding's administration. What makes this image important is that it shows how political cartoons were used to criticize dishonesty in government and how the public was losing trust in national leaders.

07

Writers and Poets

These writers represent another side of the 1920s: the cultural and literary side. The decade was not just about jazz and nightlife, but also about major changes in American writing. Authors like F. Scott Fitzgerald wrote about wealth, parties, and the emptiness behind the glamour, while writers like Langston Hughes gave voice to Black identity and creativity during the Harlem Renaissance. Together, they show that the 1920s were also a time of serious artistic growth and reflection.

08

A Speakeasy

This image shows a speakeasy, which was an illegal bar during Prohibition. Even though alcohol was banned in the 1920s, many people still drank, and speakeasies became a huge part of city nightlife. They were often hidden and secretive, but they were also social spaces where people listened to music, danced, and ignored the law. This makes speakeasies important because they show how Prohibition did not really stop drinking. Instead, it pushed it underground and helped create a culture of illegal nightlife.

09

Sports Heroes / Cultural Icons

These athletes show how sports became a huge part of popular culture in the 1920s. People followed sports more closely because of newspapers, magazines, and radio, which helped turn athletes into national celebrities. Figures like Babe Ruth became larger-than-life symbols of success, energy, and entertainment. This matters because it shows how mass media was changing American culture by making famous people more visible and more influential than ever before.

10

1920s Automobile

This car represents how technology and industry changed everyday life in the 1920s. Cars became much more common during this period, especially because companies like Ford made them cheaper and easier for ordinary people to buy. As more Americans got access to cars, travel became easier and people had more personal freedom. The automobile became one of the strongest symbols of modern life in the decade.
